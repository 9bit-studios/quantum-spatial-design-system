User-Centered Design Grounded in Purpose

Ministry websites serve diverse audiences—longtime supporters who need depth, seekers who need clarity, and donors who need transparency. Effective design means understanding these different needs and creating experiences that serve them all:

  • Accessibility as ministry: I build WCAG-compliant interfaces not just because it's required, but because accessibility is about dignity and inclusion. Semantic HTML, proper heading hierarchy, keyboard navigation, screen reader support, sufficient color contrast—these aren't checkboxes, they're how we ensure content reaches everyone, including those with disabilities.
  • Typography and hierarchy for readability: For content-driven sites, thoughtful typography is essential. I work with systematic type scales, establish clear visual hierarchy, and ensure that long-form content remains readable across devices. Good typography serves the text, making theological teaching accessible without overwhelming readers.
  • Responsive design for global reach: I design mobile-first, understanding that many users worldwide access content primarily on phones. Every layout, every interaction, every user flow is tested across devices to ensure consistent, quality experiences regardless of how people access your content.

Iterative Design and Testing Culture

You mentioned A/B testing and continuous optimization—I share that approach. Good design isn't about defending initial concepts; it's about learning what actually works:

  • I design page variants for testing with hypotheses about what will improve conversions
  • I analyze results to understand not just what performed better, but why
  • I iterate based on user feedback, analytics data, and testing insights
  • I document learnings to inform future design decisions

This approach requires humility—being willing to be wrong, to learn from data, and to serve the mission rather than my ego. That mindset aligns naturally with ministry work.
